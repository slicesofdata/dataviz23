Introduction

The mixed ANOVA compares the mean differences between groups that have been split on two independent variables. The primary purpose of a mixed ANOVA is to understand if there is an interaction between the two independent variables on the dependent variable. In ANOVA terminology, the independent variables are normally called "factors". The mixed ANOVA is often used to determine whether there are differences between independent groups over time.

SPSStop ^

What is required

In order to run a mixed ANOVA, you require the following:

» One between-subjects factor that is categorical (e.g., gender, intervention).
» One within-subjects factor that is categorical (e.g., time, condition).
» One dependent variable that is continuous (e.g., weight, cholesterol concentration).

See the Types of Variables guide for more information, if needed.

SPSStop ^

What problems can you solve using a mixed ANOVA?

A mixed ANOVA is most often used for the following procedure:

1. Determine if there is an interaction between two factors on a dependent variable

You have undertaken a study where you have measured a dependent variable over time in two or more different groups and you wish to know whether the dependent variable responded differently over time in the groups. For example, you have different participants undertaking different exercise interventions and you wish to know if cholesterol concentration reacts differently over time to the different interventions. Another way of looking at this is: Does the cholesterol concentration at the different time points depend on which group you were in?

An introduction to this tutorial

This tutorial will guide you through the process of running a mixed ANOVA with post hoc tests in SPSS, and how to interpret and report the results. It will also show you how to test all the assumptions that underlie the mixed ANOVA, how to interpret and report them, and what to do when the assumptions are violated. You will discover that a lot of the time and effort can be spent in testing these assumptions and making any necessary corrections. The repeated validation of your data caused by the testing of assumptions, making any necessary corrections to violations of assumptions, leads to an iterative process where you will run a test, correct any violations, run the test again, correct any further violations, and so on. We explain this repetitious process from the perspective of running a mixed ANOVA analysis in SPSS and guide you through the many different pathways that you might have to take to arrive at your final result.

Approaches to understanding an interaction

One of the main reasons for running a mixed ANOVA is to establish whether there is an interaction between the two factors on a dependent variable. Depending on whether the interaction is statistically significant or not will determine how, and which, results you report.

SPSStop ^

Assumptions & order of testing

For a mixed ANOVA to be able to provide a valid result, the following five assumptions must hold:

1. There are no outliers in any group (or overall).
2. The data (or residuals) are approximately normally distributed.
3. There is homogeneity of variances.
4. There is homogeneity of covariances.
5. There is sphericity.

These assumptions need to be tested so that the result from a mixed ANOVA is valid. Depending on the results of these assumptions, this guide will take you through the various options available to you in order to report your results.

These choices will be clearly signposted for you as you work through the guide.
