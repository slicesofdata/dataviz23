Introduction

The independent-samples t-test is used to determine if a difference exists between the means of two independent groups on a continuous dependent variable. More specifically, it will let you determine whether the difference between these two groups is statistically significant. This test is also known by a number of different names, including the independent t-test, independent-measures t-test, between-subjects t-test and unpaired t-test.

For example, you could use the independent-samples t-test to determine whether (mean) salaries, measured in US dollars, differed between males and females (i.e., your dependent variable would be "salary" and your independent variable would be "gender", which has two groups: "males" and "females"). You could also use an independent-samples t-test to determine whether (mean) reaction time, measured in milliseconds, differed in under 21 year olds versus those 21 years old and over (i.e., your dependent variable would be "reaction time" and your independent variable would be "age group", split into two groups: "under 21 year olds" and "21 years old and over"). We highlight three types of research design where the independent-samples t-test can be used on page 3.

If you are unfamiliar with the independent-samples t-test, you will most likely benefit from reading about its main characteristics (page 2), the research designs it can be used for (page 3) and the main considerations as you work through the test (page 4), before moving on to carrying out the test using SPSS and interpreting the results (pages 5 through 22). However, if you already understand the main characteristics of the independent-samples t-test, we would suggest starting with the example we use throughout this guide (the Example & Data Setup section on page 5). This also includes a SPSS data file you can download so that you can work through the example used in this guide (e.g., if you want to practice before carrying out the independent-samples t-test on your own data). This leads on to the data setup process in SPSS, which we cover on page 5.

If you have already set up your data correctly, you can jump to the Assumptions section starting on page 6, where we explain the six assumptions of the independent-samples t-test, including three tests of assumptions you can carry out using SPSS: (a) testing for outliers using boxplots; (b) testing for normality using the Shapiro-Wilk test of normality; and (c) testing for homogeneity of variances using Levene's test for equality of variances. On page 7, the procedure to detect outliers and test for normality is set out, before we show you how to interpret the SPSS output from these two assumptions tests, as well as explaining how to deal with violations of these assumptions (pages 8 through 11). In the Procedure section on pages 12 and 13, we guide you through the SPSS procedure required to carry out the independent-samples t-test and test for homogeneity of variances.

Finally, by the Interpreting & Reporting section, starting on page 14, you'll be in a position to start interpreting your results. How you interpret your results will depend on whether your data met or violated the assumption of homogeneity of variances. Based on the results from Levene's test of homogeneity of variances, which we explain in this section, we show you how to interpret your results and write them up on pages 15 through 22. If you simply want to interpret your results now, jump to the Interpretation & Reporting section.
